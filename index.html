Infection Control Recap

Key Principles for Dental Assistants

1. Infection Risks & Hazards

  • Sharps Injuries: From needles, scalers, burs, and other instruments.
  • Aerosols & Splatter: Generated by high-speed handpieces and ultrasonic scalers.
  • Contaminated Surfaces: Contact with clinical contact surfaces like light handles.
  • Direct Contact: Exposure to blood, saliva, and other body fluids.

2. Hierarchy of Controls

The most effective way to manage hazards is to work from the top down:

  • Elimination: Removing the hazard entirely (e.g., using digital impressions).
  • Substitution: Replacing a hazard (e.g., using safety syringes).
  • Engineering Controls: Isolating the hazard (e.g., sharps containers).
  • Administrative Controls: Changing the way people work (e.g., updated protocols).
  • PPE: The last line of defense when other controls aren't enough.

3. Control Measures

  • Hand Hygiene: The single most important measure.
  • PPE: Correct use of gloves, masks, eyewear, and gowns.
  • Instrument Reprocessing: Following validated stages of pre-cleaning, thorough manual or mechanical cleaning, drying, packaging, and sterilisation according to AS 5369.
  • Environmental Cleaning: Maintaining clinical surfaces using TGA-registered detergents for cleaning and, where indicated, hospital-grade disinfectants to achieve surface asepsis.

4. Staff Immunisation

Protecting staff and patients through proactive health measures:

  • Vaccination Records: Maintaining current records of Hepatitis B, Flu, and MMR.
  • Titre Testing: Checking for immunity levels, particularly for Hep B.
  • Annual Reviews: Ensuring boosters are up to date as per health guidelines.

5. Transmission-Based Precautions

Used in addition to Standard Precautions for patients with known or suspected communicable diseases.

  • Contact Precautions: Used for diseases spread by direct or indirect contact. Requires gloves and a gown.
  • Droplet Precautions: Used for diseases spread by large respiratory droplets. Requires a surgical mask and eye protection.
  • Airborne Precautions: Used for diseases spread by small particles that remain suspended in the air. Requires a fit-checked P2/N95 respirator.
  • Enhanced Cleaning: Increased frequency and thoroughness of cleaning environmental surfaces using TGA-registered products.

6. Breaches in Infection Control

A breach is any failure in protocol. Your response must be immediate.

  • Identify the Breach: Recognise events such as sharps injuries, body fluid splashes, PPE failures, or sterilisation processing errors.
  • Respond Safely: Perform immediate first aid (e.g., wash the site), contain the hazard, and remove yourself from further risk.
  • Report & Document: Notify your supervisor immediately and complete a formal Incident Report as per workplace policy.

7. Risk Assessment & Action

  • Assess: Evaluate the severity and patient's medical history.
  • Action: Follow the Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) workflow.
  • Document: Complete a detailed incident report.
  • Medical Advice: Seek professional clinical follow-up as required.
